🌫️
Emissions

🪟
Visibility

🚗
Body & structure
Inspect corrosion, body damage, chassis integrity and mounting points.
🔐

🔊

🔧

03
Defect Categories — The 2018 Classification System
Deep dive study
▶

In May 2018 the defect classification system changed significantly. You must understand all four categories and be able to decide which applies to a given fault. Study the grid below then complete the activities.

Dangerous
Direct and immediate risk to road safety or the environment. Vehicle must NOT be driven. Fail result — also a legal offence to drive.
Major
Likely to affect vehicle safety, have an impact on the environment, or put other road users at risk. Automatic fail — must be repaired before driving.
Minor
No significant effect on safety or the environment, but should be repaired as soon as possible. Vehicle still passes but defect is recorded.
Advisory
Not currently a defect but could become one. Tester recommends monitoring — owner should get it checked. Does not affect result.
  • For each defect category, write one example of a brake-related fault that would fall into that category (so four examples total)
  • Find out what changed between the old Pass/Fail/Advisory system and the current four-category system — why was the change made?
  • What does a Dangerous defect mean for the vehicle owner legally? Can they drive home?
  • Create a simple reference card (one side of A5) summarising all four categories with examples — something you could revise from

04
Applied Scenarios — Classify the Defect
Applied learning
▶

Read each scenario below. Decide which defect category applies (Dangerous / Major / Minor / Advisory) and write a brief justification. Use the DVSA manual to check your answers.

Scenario A
During an MOT inspection of a 2018 Ford Focus, the nearside front tyre has a tread depth of 1.0mm across the central three-quarters of the tread. The tyre has no cuts or bulges. What category would this defect be and why?
Scenario B
A 2015 VW Golf comes in for its MOT. The offside front brake pad is worn to approximately 3mm of friction material. The nearside is fine. Both discs are within tolerance. The vehicle has no braking imbalance. How would you record this?
Scenario C
A 2010 Renault Clio is on the ramp. The tester observes that the subframe has significant corrosion directly to a steering component mounting point, with visible perforations. The component moves when force is applied. What is the outcome?
Scenario D
A 2019 Toyota Yaris is presented for its first MOT. One of the rear number plate bulbs has failed — the registration plate is still fully illuminated by the remaining bulb. What would you record?
  • Write your classification and justification for each scenario — use the DVSA manual to support your reasoning
  • For any Fail scenarios, explain what the customer would need to do to get a pass
  • Discuss with a fellow learner if possible — do you agree on all four? Where might reasonable testers differ?
